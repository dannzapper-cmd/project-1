# LeadForge-Agentic Core — Email Style Guide

**Version:** 1.0  
**Purpose:** Defines tone, structure, and quality standards for all
outbound email drafts generated by the Email Drafter Agent.  
**Used by:** Email Drafter Agent, QA Evaluator Agent.  
**Dependencies:** Must be read alongside `product_knowledge.md`,
`icp_rules.md`, and `sales_playbook.md`.  
**Do not modify** without reviewing consistency with the QA
evaluation rubric and sales playbook CTAs.

---

## 1. Voice and Tone

Every email drafted by LeadForge must sound like it was written by
a **senior business development professional** — not by a marketing
automation tool, not by a junior SDR copying a template, and not by
an AI that is trying too hard to sound human.

**The voice is:**
- Calm and confident, not excited or pushy
- Specific and grounded, not vague or generic
- Respectful of the reader's time and intelligence
- Direct without being abrupt
- Professional without being formal to the point of stiffness

**The tone is NOT:**
- Enthusiastic or hyperbolic
- Familiar or casual beyond what the context warrants
- Apologetic or overly deferential
- Urgency-driven or pressure-based
- Feature-list oriented

**The mental model for tone:**
Write as if a thoughtful colleague is reaching out to someone they
respect, with a specific reason for the conversation, and genuine
interest in whether it is relevant — not as if a salesperson is
trying to book a meeting at any cost.

## 2. Email Length Target

| Component | Target Length |
|---|---|
| Subject line | 6–10 words |
| Full email body | 100–160 words |
| Opening line | 1–2 sentences |
| Pain hypothesis | 2–3 sentences |
| Value proposition | 2–3 sentences |
| CTA | 1 sentence |
| Sign-off | 1 line |

**Hard limits:**
- Never exceed 200 words in the email body.
- Never write a subject line longer than 12 words.
- Never write more than 4 paragraphs.
- Each paragraph should be 1–3 sentences maximum.

**Why brevity matters:**
B2B buyers at the VP and Director level receive dozens of outreach
emails per week. An email that requires more than 30 seconds to read
will not be read. Length is not a signal of effort — it is a signal
of poor prioritization.

## 3. Email Structure

Every email must follow this exact four-part structure.
Do not reorder. Do not skip a section. Do not merge sections.

---

### Part 1: Relevant Context (Opening)

**Purpose:** Establish why this email is not random. Ground the
outreach in something specific and observable about the company
or its situation.

**Rules:**
- Must reference at least one specific signal from the research context.
- Must not fabricate a source or claim to have visited the website
  unless the research context explicitly supports it.
- Must not open with a compliment, a greeting about the weather,
  or a statement about how impressive the company is.
- Must not open with "I came across your company."
- Must not open with "Hope this finds you well."
- One to two sentences maximum.

**Good opening pattern:**
> "[Company name] appears to be [specific observable fact or signal].
> Given [what that implies about their situation], I wanted to reach out."

**What counts as a valid context anchor:**
- A hiring signal (e.g., "you're scaling your SDR team")
- A growth signal (e.g., "following your recent expansion into [market]")
- An industry pattern (e.g., "logistics companies entering new regions
  typically face a specific research challenge at this stage")
- A role-specific observation (e.g., "RevOps teams managing a growing
  SDR headcount often hit a qualification consistency wall")

**What does NOT count as a valid context anchor:**
- "I saw your website" without a specific observation
- "Your company is doing great things" — vague, unverifiable
- A fabricated news item or signal not present in the research context
- A generic industry statement that applies to every company equally

---

### Part 2: Pain Hypothesis

**Purpose:** Name the specific operational problem that LeadForge
addresses, framed as a hypothesis relevant to this company's situation.

**Rules:**
- Must connect to one of the five core pains from `sales_playbook.md`.
- Must be stated as a hypothesis, not as a confirmed fact.
  Use language like "often," "typically," "at this stage," "in our
  experience" — not "you definitely have this problem."
- Must be specific to the company's industry, size, or signals.
- Must not be a generic pain statement that applies to every company
  in every industry.
- Two to three sentences maximum.

**Good pain hypothesis pattern:**
> "At [company type] companies scaling outbound at your stage, the
> research and qualification bottleneck often becomes the constraint —
> not the number of reps. Each new SDR adds research demand faster
> than it adds pipeline capacity, and qualification consistency
> starts to vary across the team."

**Bad pain hypothesis pattern:**
> "Many companies struggle with their sales process and waste time
> on leads that don't convert."

The bad version applies to every company in existence. It says nothing
specific. It will be ignored.

---

### Part 3: Value Proposition

**Purpose:** State what LeadForge does and why it is relevant to
this specific company's situation. Connect the product directly to
the pain hypothesis stated in Part 2.

**Rules:**
- Must use only approved claims from `product_knowledge.md`.
- Must not introduce features or capabilities not defined in the
  product knowledge file.
- Must connect explicitly to the pain stated in Part 2 —
  not introduce a new, unrelated benefit.
- Must not use superlatives: "best," "fastest," "most powerful,"
  "revolutionary," "game-changing."
- Must not claim guaranteed outcomes: "will increase your reply rate,"
  "will double your pipeline."
- Must explain the product in plain business language — no jargon,
  no AI buzzwords.
- Two to three sentences maximum.

**Good value proposition pattern:**
> "LeadForge runs each lead through a structured pipeline —
> validation, research, ICP scoring, strategy, and draft —
> and presents everything to your team for review before any
> outreach goes out. Every qualification decision is explained,
> every email is grounded in company-specific context,
> and the human stays in control at every step."

**Bad value proposition pattern:**
> "Our cutting-edge AI platform leverages advanced machine learning
> to supercharge your sales pipeline and revolutionize the way
> your team prospects."

The bad version is meaningless. It could describe any AI tool
from the last five years. It creates distrust, not interest.

---

### Part 4: Soft CTA

**Purpose:** Invite a specific, low-commitment next step.

**Rules:**
- Must use only approved CTAs from `sales_playbook.md`.
- Must be a single sentence.
- Must ask a question or propose a specific action —
  not leave the next step ambiguous.
- Must match the evidence level of the lead:
  high-evidence leads can receive a slightly more direct CTA;
  low-evidence leads must receive a softer, more exploratory CTA.
- Must not create urgency, scarcity, or pressure.
- Must not imply that a commitment has already been made.
- Must not include a link, a calendar booking URL, or a
  "click here" instruction in the draft — the human reviewer
  adds those before sending.

**Approved CTA patterns:**
> "Would a 15-minute conversation make sense to see if this fits
> your current workflow?"

> "Does this match anything on your current ops roadmap?"

> "Happy to share how a similar team approached this —
> worth a quick look?"

**CTA calibration by evidence level:**

| Evidence Level | CTA Approach |
|---|---|
| High (2+ strong signals) | Specific meeting request with clear time estimate |
| Medium (1 strong or 2 moderate signals) | Open question about relevance or fit |
| Low (weak signals only) | Exploratory question — "does this resonate?" style |
| Insufficient evidence | Do not draft a CTA. Flag for human review. |

## 4. Subject Line Rules

The subject line is the single most important line in the email.
If it does not earn an open, nothing else matters.

**Rules:**
- Must be 6–10 words. Hard limit: 12 words.
- Must not use ALL CAPS anywhere in the subject line.
- Must not use exclamation marks.
- Must not use spam-trigger patterns: "FREE," "URGENT," "Limited time,"
  "Act now," "Don't miss."
- Must not be a question — subject lines as questions perform poorly
  in B2B outbound.
- Must reference something specific to the company or their situation.
- Must not be a generic category statement:
  ❌ "Improving your sales process"
  ❌ "AI for your team"
  ❌ "Quick question"
- Should create curiosity or relevance without being clickbait.
- Should sound like it came from a colleague, not a marketing campaign.

**Approved subject line patterns:**

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| Signal + implication | "Scaling SDR teams without the research bottleneck" |
| Role + specific problem | "Pipeline qualification consistency for RevOps teams" |
| Company stage + challenge | "Outbound research at Series B scale" |
| Specific pain + product hint | "Qualification before CRM entry — a different approach" |
| Industry + operational pattern | "How logistics teams are prioritizing new market accounts" |

**Subject lines to reject at QA:**
- Any subject line that could apply to any company in any industry
- Any subject line with a superlative or hype word
- Any subject line that overpromises an outcome
- Any subject line that uses the word "AI" as the primary hook —
  it triggers skepticism, not curiosity, in B2B buyers

## 5. Personalization Rules

Personalization is the difference between an email that gets read
and an email that gets deleted. But personalization must be grounded
in real evidence — not performed.

**Levels of personalization:**

| Level | Definition | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| Deep | References a specific, verifiable signal unique to this company | High-evidence leads with 2+ strong signals |
| Contextual | References a pattern specific to this company's industry, size, and stage | Medium-evidence leads |
| Structural | Uses the correct persona framing and industry language, but no unique signals | Low-evidence leads — flag for human review |
| None | No company-specific context available | Do not draft. Flag as insufficient for outreach. |

**Rules for using research signals in personalization:**
- Only reference signals that are present in the `company_research.json`
  context for this lead.
- Do not use signals from other leads in the same dataset.
- Do not reference a signal as confirmed fact if it is marked
  `confidence: low` in the research context — use hedged language.
- If a signal is marked `confidence: medium`, use it with
  appropriate qualification: "it appears," "based on available context."
- If a signal is marked `confidence: high`, it can be stated
  more directly — but never as absolute certainty.

**Forbidden personalization patterns:**
- ❌ "I saw on your website that..." — unless the research context
  explicitly documents a website finding with high confidence
- ❌ "I noticed you recently..." — unless the specific event is
  documented in the research context
- ❌ "I've been following [company name] for a while..." —
  false familiarity, never acceptable
- ❌ Using the contact's first name more than once in the email body —
  it reads as a mail merge artifact, not genuine familiarity

## 6. Evidence Usage Rules

Evidence is what makes an email specific. These rules define how
evidence from the research context can and cannot be used.

**Rule 1: Only use evidence that is present in the research context.**
Do not invent company facts, news items, hiring announcements,
or signals that are not documented in `company_research.json`
for this specific lead.

**Rule 2: Match language confidence to evidence confidence.**

| Research Confidence | Language to use in email |
|---|---|
| high | "Given your recent expansion into..." |
| medium | "Based on available context, it appears that..." |
| low | Do not use in the email body. Mention in strategy notes only. |

**Rule 3: Do not cite sources in the email body.**
Emails are not research reports. Do not write "according to LinkedIn"
or "as reported by TechCrunch." Reference the observation, not the source.

**Rule 4: Do not stack evidence.**
One well-chosen signal used specifically is stronger than three weak
signals listed together. Pick the most relevant and most confident
signal and build the opening around it.

**Rule 5: If no usable evidence exists, do not fabricate.**
Write a structurally correct email at the Structural personalization
level and flag it explicitly for human review with a note:
"Low evidence — recommend additional research before sending."

## 7. Claims to Avoid

These claims are forbidden in all email drafts. They either overclaim
product capabilities, create legal risk, or damage credibility.

- ❌ Any claim from the Forbidden Product Claims list in
  `product_knowledge.md`
- ❌ "Our AI will..." — implies automation of outcomes the system
  does not guarantee
- ❌ "Guaranteed results" or any variation
- ❌ "We've helped companies like [specific real company name]" —
  do not reference real companies without documented permission
- ❌ "This email was written specifically for you" —
  implies manual effort that was not performed
- ❌ "I've reviewed your website in detail" — unless the research
  context explicitly documents this
- ❌ Any claim that the email has already been reviewed and approved
  by a human — this is the draft stage
- ❌ Any claim about the buyer's internal data, revenue, or
  performance metrics unless documented in research context

## 8. Words and Phrases to Avoid

These patterns trigger immediate skepticism in B2B buyers.
The Email Drafter Agent must detect and eliminate them.

**Hype and enthusiasm:**
- "Exciting," "game-changing," "revolutionary," "disruptive,"
  "cutting-edge," "innovative," "best-in-class," "world-class"

**Fake urgency:**
- "Don't miss out," "limited time," "act now," "before it's too late,"
  "only a few spots left"

**Empty filler openers:**
- "Hope this finds you well," "I hope you're having a great week,"
  "Quick question for you," "Just reaching out to..."

**False familiarity:**
- "As you know," "I'm sure you've noticed," "I've been following
  your work," "I've been a fan of [company] for years"

**Aggressive commitment language:**
- "When can we connect?", "I'll follow up on Thursday if I don't
  hear back," "I've blocked time on my calendar for you"

**AI and tech buzzwords:**
- "Leverage," "utilize," "synergy," "robust," "scalable solution,"
  "AI-powered," "machine learning-driven," "end-to-end"

**Weak closing language:**
- "Let me know your thoughts," "Feel free to reach out,"
  "Looking forward to hearing from you," "Thanks in advance"

## 9. Good Email Example

**Lead context:** B2B SaaS company, 140 employees, US, VP of Revenue
Operations, hiring 3 SDRs, recently closed Series B.

---

**Subject:** Qualification consistency as your SDR team scales

**Body:**

Scaling an SDR team after a Series B round typically surfaces a
specific operations problem before it surfaces a revenue problem:
research and qualification start to vary by rep faster than
onboarding can correct for it.

For RevOps teams at this stage, the gap usually shows up in pipeline
quality — not in headcount. More reps means more manual research,
and manual research means inconsistent scoring.

LeadForge runs each lead through a structured pipeline — validation,
ICP scoring, research, strategy, and a reviewed email draft —
and presents everything for human approval before outreach goes out.
Every qualification decision is explained. Nothing is automated
past the recommendation stage.

Would a 15-minute conversation make sense to see if this fits
your current workflow?

[Name] | LeadForge

---

**Why this email works:**
- Opening references a specific, observable signal (Series B + SDR hiring)
- Pain hypothesis is specific to RevOps at Series B SaaS scale
- Value proposition uses only approved claims, no buzzwords
- CTA is soft, specific, and time-bounded
- 152 words — within the 100–160 target
- No fake familiarity, no urgency, no hype

## 10. Bad Email Example

**Same lead context as above.**

---

**Subject:** Revolutionize your sales pipeline with AI!

**Body:**

Hope this finds you well! I came across your company and was
really impressed by what you're building.

I wanted to reach out because we have an exciting new AI-powered
platform that helps companies like yours leverage cutting-edge
machine learning to supercharge their sales process and drive
revenue growth.

Our solution has helped hundreds of companies increase their
pipeline by 3x and reduce prospecting time by 60%. I know
your team would see incredible results.

I'd love to jump on a quick call — when are you free this week?

Looking forward to connecting!

[Name]

---

**Why this email fails:**
- Subject line uses hype and an exclamation mark
- Opens with an empty greeting and vague compliment
- "I came across your company" — fabricated familiarity
- "AI-powered platform," "cutting-edge machine learning,"
  "supercharge" — pure buzzwords with no meaning
- "Hundreds of companies," "3x pipeline," "60% reduction" —
  unverifiable claims
- "I'd love to jump on a quick call — when are you free this week?"
  — aggressive, presumes availability
- Zero personalization specific to this company or contact
- Could have been sent to any company in any industry

## 11. QA Checklist for Email Quality

The QA Evaluator Agent must verify every email draft against
this checklist before it reaches human review.

**Structure:**
- [ ] Email follows the four-part structure: Context, Pain, Value, CTA
- [ ] Subject line is 6–10 words, no caps, no exclamation marks
- [ ] Email body is 100–160 words (hard limit: 200)
- [ ] No paragraph exceeds 3 sentences
- [ ] CTA is a single sentence

**Personalization:**
- [ ] Opening references at least one specific signal from the
  research context (for high/medium evidence leads)
- [ ] No signals used that are not present in the research context
- [ ] Confidence language matches the evidence confidence level
- [ ] Contact's first name is used once or not at all

**Claims and tone:**
- [ ] No forbidden product claims from `product_knowledge.md`
- [ ] No hype words or buzzwords from the avoid list
- [ ] No fake urgency or pressure language
- [ ] No fake familiarity openers
- [ ] No unverifiable statistics or outcome guarantees
- [ ] Value proposition connects directly to the pain hypothesis stated

**CTA:**
- [ ] CTA is from the approved list in `sales_playbook.md`
- [ ] CTA matches the evidence level of the lead
- [ ] CTA does not imply a commitment already made
- [ ] No calendar links, booking URLs, or "click here" instructions

**Safety:**
- [ ] No real email addresses included
- [ ] No real personal contact details included
- [ ] No claim that the email has already been sent or approved
- [ ] No fabricated sources or invented company facts

## 12. Rules for Low-Evidence Leads

When the research context for a lead is marked `low_evidence`
or `partial`, the Email Drafter Agent must follow these rules:

1. **Downgrade to Structural personalization level.**
   Use industry and role-appropriate language, but do not reference
   specific signals that are not documented.

2. **Do not fabricate signals to compensate.**
   A structurally correct email with honest uncertainty is better
   than a confident email built on invented context.

3. **Use the most conservative approved CTA.**
   "Does this match anything on your current ops roadmap?"
   is appropriate for low-evidence leads.

4. **Add a draft warning note** visible to the human reviewer:
   "Low evidence — recommend additional research before approving
   this draft for outreach."

5. **Do not block the draft entirely** unless the lead has zero
   usable context. A partial draft with an honest warning is more
   useful to the human reviewer than a blank output.

6. **Flag the specific missing evidence** that would upgrade the
   draft quality: "A confirmed contact role, company size, or
   one specific signal would allow a more targeted opening."

## 13. Rules for Not Overclaiming

Overclaiming is the most damaging failure mode for outbound email
quality. It damages sender credibility, creates compliance risk,
and trains buyers to ignore the sender's future emails.

**The overclaiming test:**
Before including any claim in an email draft, the agent must be able
to answer "yes" to both of these questions:

1. Is this claim supported by evidence in the research context
   for this specific lead?
2. Is this claim present in the Approved Product Claims list
   in `product_knowledge.md`?

If either answer is "no," the claim must be removed or rewritten.

**Rewriting overclaims:**

| Overclaim | Safe Rewrite |
|---|---|
| "We've helped companies like yours increase pipeline 3x" | "The approach is designed to improve qualification consistency — results depend on how it fits your current workflow" |
| "Our AI is the most accurate qualification tool available" | "The qualification logic is explainable and consistent — every score has a documented reason" |
| "I know this is a challenge for your team" | "At companies at your stage, this challenge typically appears before it becomes visible in the numbers" |
| "This will save your team hours every week" | "The research and qualification steps are designed to reduce the manual burden per lead" |
| "Your competitors are already using this" | Remove entirely — unverifiable and manipulative |
